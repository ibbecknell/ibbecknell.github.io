Common Types of Mushrooms

Below is a list of popular wild mushrooms located in the Pacific Northwest and what to look for when you are mushroom hunting.

Why Should I identify wild mushrooms?

Wild mushrooms have great health benefits. They can improve your skin; boost your energy levels, lower cholesterol, and are packed with vitamins and minerals. Some mushrooms treat wounds, and some even have been said to prevent cancer growth. And on top of all of that, there are mushrooms that are used as a remedy to help with arthritis and inhibit diabetes.

Identifying wild mushrooms

There are four main body parts to most mushrooms:

  • Cap: the "hat" or umbrella-shaped top of the mushroom sitting on the stalk.
  • Gills: what lives on the underside of the cap and releases spores.
  • Stalk: what the mushroom cap stands on.
  • Mycelium: the roots that push the mushroom up for spore dispersal.

The Foolproof Four

Identifying wild mushrooms requires awareness and understanding of their parts. There are many poisonous look-a-likes. The "foolproof four" are some of the best species to start with for edible mushroom identification. The following mushrooms: giant puffballs, morel mushrooms, chicken of the woods, and the chanterelles, are the easiest to identify.

Chanterelle (Cantharellus cibarius)

The chanterelle mushroom looks like a curled vessel, and is known as a delicacy. With white to yellow flesh, they have a fruity smell.

Cap: orange to yellowish with wavy or downward curled edges and some edges turned up. The middle of the mushroom can often dip inwards.

Gills: Underside is smooth, wrinkled or lined with thick light orange to yellow gills with narrow depth. Veins are interconnected.

Stalk: light orange to pale yellow stalk, tapering at the bottom.

Spores: yellow

Size: Medium to large when they reach maturity.

Season: Fall-early spring

Habitat: Can be found on the ground in open woods and forests and often under trees.

Range: Western United States and Alaska.

Morel (Morchella esculenta)

Morel mushrooms are some of the most prized edible mushrooms. They are known for their honeycomb cap.

Cap: brown cap, round to oval or conical. Cap has a wavy honeycomb look with darker brown holes throughout.

Gills: gills not visible

Stalk: tan colored stalk; hollow.

Spores: No sac at the stalk's base.

Size: 1 - 12 inches tall

Season: springtime

Habitat: On the ground in woods, forests, sandy soils, and near rivers and streams; wet and damp places.

Range: North America

Western Giant Puffball (Calvatia booniana)

This giant puffball looks like a puffed ball. It is round in shape.

Cap: This thick-skinned; white with white to light brown textured marks all over.

Gills: gills not visible

Stalk: No stalk.

Spores: inside round sacks

Size: up to 10 inches or more in diameter.

Season: summer - fall

Habitat: grassy fields, hillsides, and open areas, lawns, on rotting wood and near roads.

Range: North America

Sulfur Shelf "Chicken of the Woods" (Laetiporus sulphureus)

Many say this mushroom tastes like chicken.

Cap: Yellow to orange shelf

Gills: bright yellow

Stalk: very short stubby stalk

Spores: white

Size: 2 to 20 inches

Season: mid to late summer - fall

Habitat: at the base or moving up high on the tree bark of trees such as oak, beech, yew, willow, cherry and chestnut.

Range: North America
